# Skill: Customer Behavioural Profile, A Leading GCC Operator

## What this profile is

Ericsson's captured read of how this operator actually decides, negotiates, and behaves over time, built from years across the table. The operator is not a monolith. It is a cast of internal actors with conflicting agendas, and every behaviour below is attributed to the actor who drives it. Use it to reason about who wants what, who really decides, and what the customer is doing in a given move, not to imitate a generic operator.

---

## The internal cast

- **Board, sovereign or government shareholders, regulator, security agencies.** Carry national vision, sovereignty, security, and prestige. The top of the stack.
- **CEO.** Carries alignment with national and group strategy, and the reputational lens.
- **CTIO/CTO, network and IT VPs, domain heads.** Carry network quality, technology leadership, and migration-risk aversion. Hold a real veto on risk grounds.
- **CFO and board finance committees.** Carry EBITDA, cash, and TCO over eight to ten years. Hold a real veto on economic grounds.
- **CPO and procurement.** Carry demonstrable savings versus last cycle and a visible competitive process. Run the machinery, rarely initiate.

## What the operator optimizes for

On paper, EBITDA, network leadership, and digital transformation. In reality a stack of objectives with different owners.

- **National vision and sovereignty** (board, sovereign fund, regulator, security): align with national programmes (5G, AI, cloud, smart cities, defence and public-safety networks), keep strategic control of core infrastructure and security posture, and be seen as a regional technology leader rather than a follower.
- **Regulatory standing and political risk** (regulator, ministries, board risk committees): avoid sanctions, outages, security incidents, and data-protection failures, and be the trusted national operator for critical infrastructure. Drives strong sensitivity to EoL/EoS, supportability, and the ability to comply with evolving data-protection and residency law.
- **Network quality and technology leadership** (CTIO, network and IT VPs): hold or gain a top-three benchmark position and support the promised services (B2B, MCN and defence, FWA). Resists perceived migration or performance risk and unproven vendors where a failure would be visible.
- **Profitability and capital discipline** (CFO, CEO, finance committees): hit EBITDA and cash targets under real margin pressure and limited pricing power, and avoid long-term cost traps such as locked-in hardware and legacy platforms. Drives aggressive TCO over eight to ten years and tough payment terms, warranties, and LDs.
- **Procurement savings** (CPO, procurement): show savings versus last cycle and a defensible competitive process for the board and regulator. Drives benchmarking, multi-vendor competition, MFN, and reuse of concessions across opcos.

When these collide, the priority stack in a real crunch:

1. Sovereignty, security, and national-vision obligations.
2. Avoiding visible failure or scandal.
3. Prestige projects and leadership narratives.
4. Medium-term TCO and financial discipline.
5. Procurement savings and deal-by-deal unit price.

They will accept more cost, complexity, or slower ROI for national-priority coverage, strategic independence from disfavoured vendors, or visible leadership. Delivery certainty and strategic fit beat list price alone.

## How a big vendor decision actually gets made

Externally it looks like RFP, evaluation, award. Internally it is layered.

- **Pre-deal shaping (months to years before the RFP).** CTIO and domain heads engage vendors through PoCs, labs, and country-strategy workshops to test capability and build comfort on migration risk. Government and regulators set direction through spectrum awards, coverage obligations, and vendor and security policy. The board and sovereign shareholders set long-term themes (reduce dependency on certain vendors, privatize or IPO, invest in specific verticals). By the time the RFP lands, technical options are heavily pre-filtered, and a "neutral" RFP often reflects this pre-work.
- **The formal path.** Technical and commercial working groups bench proposals and model TCO over eight to ten years. Technical and investment committees test security, regulatory fit, and hurdle rates against national-vision KPIs. The CTIO-CFO-CEO triad each apply their lens: technically safe and future-proof, economically acceptable, strategically and reputationally aligned. For mega-deals (core, MCN and defence, IPO-sensitive investment, foreign-vendor swaps) the board, sovereign shareholder, and government can delay or reshape the deal, often tied to wider industrial policy such as positions on Chinese versus Western vendors.
- **Consensus and reversibility.** Consensus is managed, not organic. Technical teams narrow to one or two credible vendors, finance and strategy press TCO and risk, and the CEO and board choose between two acceptable options, rarely many. Once the board or sovereign shareholder has blessed a strategic choice, it is sticky for a five-to-seven-year cycle but not irreversible: they keep a second vendor warm and may rebalance volumes at the next refresh. The pattern is visible in a recent multi-domain group award where the operator consciously kept the incumbent at roughly 60% RAN share rather than 100%.
- **Real veto versus apparent veto.** It appears to sit with procurement (signs LOIs, runs RFPs) and engineering (writes vendor rankings). It really sits with the CTIO (can kill a vendor on migration or risk grounds), the CFO (can declare a deal economically untenable under group TCO scrutiny), and the board, sovereign shareholder, or regulator (can override on sovereignty, security, or group vendor policy). A vendor that wins on paper can lose in the last mile to any of these.

## Recurring negotiation plays

- **Multi-vendor competition and benchmarking.** Always keep at least two credible vendors live into the late stages, an incumbent against a Western or Chinese challenger. Ask for best-and-final repeatedly and quote competitor economics implicitly ("we have 40% better TCO from competition"). The pattern across win/loss is benchmarking used to compress incumbent margin even when they ultimately stay with the incumbent.
- **Timing pressure.** Anchor on hard external dates (spectrum-licence conditions, EoL/EoM deadlines, IPO windows) and manufacture internal ones ("signature before Ramadan," "the board meets on X," "budget freeze"). They allow months of technical dialogue, then compress commercial negotiation into weeks to extract last-minute price cuts and T&C concessions ("if you do not sign now, we cannot take you to the board").
- **Playing vendors against each other.** Signal different things to different vendors and leak partial truth ("others will prime the 3PP cloud," "the challenger gave us a ten-year TCO and 270-day payments"). Rarely outright bluff, more often selective truth: the competitive commitments are usually real, presented in the most pressuring way.
- **Reuse of concessions.** Every concession ever given (a longer warranty, a specific LD construct, MFN wording, 270-day terms) becomes the baseline for the next deal and sometimes for other opcos.

Once you know the plays they are predictable. Expect repeated asks on payment terms, warranties, the ten-year TCO view, and benchmarking against a named competitor, and expect them to keep a low-price, high-risk option (appliance, heavy customisation) in the pocket for leverage without always taking it in the end.

## Loyalty versus opportunism over time

Pragmatic, and remembers everything.

- **Incumbents.** Valued for execution trust and lower migration risk, and for understanding the network, the political environment, and the regulatory obligations. But not sentimental: a visible failure (RAN outage, core delay, security incident) immediately elevates challengers and becomes an internal narrative ("we must reduce single-vendor dependency"). Incumbents lose when they stay rigid on commercial models, lifecycle, or cost.
- **Memory and standing.** They remember failures (contract disputes, poor incident handling, broken roadmap promises) and favours (support during crises, going beyond contract in national emergencies, accepting short-term pain to help them meet a government obligation). In the next major deal a vendor with strong execution history and crisis support gets a softer starting point on trust and access even without the lowest price, while a vendor with a big past failure becomes the risky choice that needs deeper technical and stakeholder justification.
- **Segmented, not either-or.** For core strategic domains (5G core, MCN, national backbone) they lean to trust and continuity with pressure: work the incumbent hard on price and innovation but avoid risky swaps unless truly justified. For peripheral or commoditised domains (some IT stacks, non-critical services) they behave opportunistically, switching on price or following global sourcing trends. Behaviour in strategic crises matters more for long-term standing than any single discount.

## Reading real intent

- **Serious and undecided.** Investing time in deep technical workshops (traffic models, deployment scenarios, MCN and UCC architectures) and senior attention in design reviews. Sharing real traffic models and constraints, not just headline subscriber numbers. Pushing both vendors on technical and commercial fronts, and involving regulatory and security in the evaluation, not just procurement.
- **Already decided.** An RFP suddenly carrying one vendor's language (architecture blocks, product code-names). Access limited to procurement and mid-level engineers, with strategic sessions at CTIO, CFO, or CEO level refused despite repeated requests. Being asked to "just submit a compliant offer" with no serious engagement, and deviation asks that align suspiciously with a competitor's known risk appetite. The winner was often lined up through pre-work, group frameworks, or a political decision, and the RFP is used to validate or pressure it.
- **Using you as leverage.** Very RFP-driven with minimal iterative design. Questions heavy on price and T&Cs and light on migration or risk. Repeated "last and final" discount asks with no progress on technical or governance discussion, and no real visibility into the decision forums. The pattern in win/loss is an Ericsson presence that decompresses incumbent pricing without changing the final choice, which reverts to incumbency and perceived migration risk.
- **Archetype-specific misreads.** Long silent periods read as a dead deal are often internal coordination (regulator, board) or waiting for a macro trigger (spectrum, fiscal year, IPO step). Mixed messages, procurement saying "technically number one, last commercially" while the technical team says "we need your solution," are usually genuine internal CTIO-versus-CFO conflict, not manipulation.

## What an outsider with only public information gets wrong

- **Overestimating Western-style procurement logic.** The RFPs, RFQs, and procurement policies look European, but decisions are filtered through national strategy, sovereignty, and board politics. Formal procurement is a necessary layer, while pre-RFP shaping, government positions on specific vendors, and sovereign-shareholder priorities set the direction. Outsiders over-weight the scoring sheet and under-weight the political steering.
- **Underestimating sovereignty, data sensitivity, and security.** "Cloud is cloud, data is data, EU and US patterns apply" is wrong here. Sovereignty over data, lawful intercept, and key infrastructure is not negotiable. AI tools processing data abroad, hyperscaler hosting without regulator comfort, and foreign remote support without strict controls are all politically exposed. Investor-report cloud and AI hype mislead. On the ground, data location and control are tightly scrutinised.
- **Misreading sovereign and family ownership and prestige projects.** The operator is often majority-owned by a sovereign fund, ruling-family interests, or government entities, and flagship projects (5G SA, MCN and defence, smart cities) are part political and part industrial policy. An outsider sees a loss-making prestige project as poor strategy. In fact it is investment in political capital and sovereignty, and vendors who help it succeed earn disproportionate trust for future monetisable deals.
- **Misjudging timing and cycles.** Ramadan, Hajj, national holidays, and fiscal and regulatory cycles drive decision timelines, board meetings, and stakeholder availability. Delays read as lack of interest are often decisions batched around national cycles such as budget approvals, national-plan milestones, and regulatory reporting.
- **Taking official narratives at face value.** The public narrative of open competition, vendor neutrality, and purely commercial decisions hides group-level and government positions on Chinese versus Western vendors, on cloud versus on-prem, and on defence and public-safety architectures, all of which steer outcomes. A generic model over-weights price and technology and under-weights geopolitics, security, and national vision.
