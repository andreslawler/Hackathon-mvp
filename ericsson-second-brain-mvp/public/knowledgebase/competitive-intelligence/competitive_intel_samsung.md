**Competitive Intelligence - Samsung**

**Profile and position**

Samsung Networks positions itself as a **challenger RAN vendor**, using aggressive entries in Europe and North America and selective engagement in high-ARPU and strategic markets. Its global RAN revenue share fell to around **5%** in 2024 with RAN sales down roughly **30%**, driven by declines in North America and Asia Pacific.

Samsung holds around **20** 5G commercial contracts, with about **15** live networks, with install base concentrated in South East Asia and North America and a single confirmed European 5G RAN footprint. Core focus markets are **North America, Korea and Japan**. Europe has moved from pilots to early deployments, while **MEA and Latin America remain deprioritised**. India is mixed: Samsung lost a major 5G RAN award at one Tier 1 operator despite LTE incumbency, but won about **10%** 5G RAN share at another.

From an analyst perspective, Samsung is framed as a **major challenger** in Open RAN and vRAN, advocating virtualisation and disaggregation to differentiate from Ericsson and other Tier-1 vendors. Its 5G install base (low/mid/mmWave, excluding China) is small versus Ericsson but visible, with global RAN share in the mid-single digits and stronger weight in SEA and US/Canada than in Europe or MEA.

**Portfolio and technical position**

Samsung's RAN strategy is built around **Open RAN, vRAN and cloud-native architectures**. It supplies O-RAN compliant vRAN for Tier-1 deployments such as KDDI Japan and Liberty Puerto Rico, and provides vRAN plus traditional RAN in a large multi-market Vodafone deal spanning India and selected European markets. It is a primary Open RAN supplier to DISH in the US and Vodafone in Europe in recent periods.

On **baseband**, Samsung has removed its higher-capacity CDU60 from the roadmap and focuses on the CDU50 plus a new compact 1U "baseband lite" platform and COTS-based vDU, including a lite 1U baseband tailored for India. Key takeaways in Ericsson CI note relatively **limited headroom for capacity growth**, and **50-100% higher baseband power consumption** versus Ericsson for comparable configurations.

In **radios and M-MIMO**, Ericsson analysis concludes we hold a technology advantage at the top end of the Massive MIMO TDD portfolio and are "clearly ahead" in FDD remote radio footprint and output power for similar configurations. Our remote radio footprint is about **25% better** for similar output power and we maintain about **10-15%** better power efficiency in comparable classes, while a specific Samsung dual-band tri-sector radio optimised for an Indian operator is acknowledged as strong on size, weight and fronthaul interface.

On **AI, 5G-Advanced and Cloud RAN**, Samsung's roadmap is explicit:

- AI: it offers anomaly detection, Energy Saving Manager and Load Balancing Manager as standard AI functions in Non-RT RIC, plus a broader ecosystem via partners such as NVIDIA, VIAVI and AWS.
- 5G-Advanced: Samsung has showcased FR1 SA 6CC trials with a 5G-Advanced modem and vRAN in a Tier-1 US network, reaching around **5.5 Gbps** peak speeds, and has demonstrated multi-vendor RIC functionality with a US operator and Qualcomm.
- Cloud RAN: it pushes a COTS-based vDU/vCU and cloud RAN proposition with COTS plus PaaS, including failed "sell with / sell through" attempts in Japan that still illustrate its preferred model.
- Open RAN: Samsung positions itself as an Open RAN reference vendor, with commercial O-RAN deployments and pilots in Japan, North America, Europe and Latin America, often combining O-RAN radios with cloud-native vRAN.

Overall, Ericsson CI concludes that **portfolio composition remains the key driver** in Open RAN engagements and that Ericsson retains a structural **TCO and energy advantage** even where Samsung is strong in vRAN and Open RAN.

**Commercial and pricing behaviour**

Samsung's commercial strategy is **unconventional and highly flexible**. It deliberately avoids a single standard commercial model, instead tailoring offers to each RFx and aligning closely to the customer's commercial strategy, bundling and pricing per deal. As a new entrant, Samsung typically **anchors bids with a relatively low initial price** and then uses flexibility on scope, bundling and commercial structure to meet its margin objectives.

It **cherry-picks** primarily **high-ARPU markets for margin** and **high-volume markets for scale**, with Europe framed as an active break-in region and MEA and Latin America consciously deprioritised in the near term. Samsung also uses selective cross-selling between devices and infrastructure to strengthen or buy market entry where that combination is attractive to the operator.

Software is a core commercial lever. In Korea and Japan, Samsung charges a **fixed annual software development fee** per network, renegotiated yearly, covering development, implementation and lifecycle management of customer-specific features. Software licences are typically **bundled into baseband unit price or the development fee**, with flexible models such as pay-as-you-grow or per-release pricing. Samsung does not assign explicit price tags to individual software features, which gives freedom to move value between hardware and software objects.

On hardware, Samsung has started to introduce **hardware capacity (HWAC) price objects** in 5G, especially on radios, while baseband still has no HWAC concept. In 5G Massive MIMO, Ericsson applies HWAC for both radio and baseband, while Samsung keeps baseband as pure hardware and in some cases sells MIMO layers as HWAC for active antennas.

Ericsson CI benchmarked TCO and found a **TCO gap up to about 20% in Ericsson's favour**, driven largely by Samsung needing more equipment to meet requirements and **up to three times higher 5G power consumption**. At the same time, Samsung's internal benchmarks and CU feedback indicate it **will price competitively but not deliberately undercut below perceived market levels**, supported by a good understanding of competitor price points and the ability to re-allocate price between HW and SW.

However, Ericsson's weekly Telco Signals notes one recent **Costa Rican 5G tender** where a consortium formally challenged Samsung's bid as "ruinous", pointing to an offer of about **0.8 million USD** for 5G core installation versus an internal estimate around **5.6 million USD**, combined with alleged non-compliance on 4G-5G interoperability. Ericsson also sought Samsung's disqualification in that process. This highlights that, in some cases, Samsung can be perceived as using **very aggressive price anchors** to break into strategic accounts.

**Where they win and where they lose against Ericsson**

Samsung wins where **Open RAN and vRAN** are central to the deal narrative and where operators want a **multi-vendor, cloud-native RAN** with a strong challenger component. Key examples in Ericsson CI include:

- A nationwide O-RAN compliant vRAN deal with **KDDI in Japan**, combining 4G and 5G vRAN on general-purpose servers in a commercial-scale Open RAN deployment.
- A large multi-market RAN modernisation with **Vodafone**, spanning India and European markets with vRAN plus traditional RAN and energy-efficient M-MIMO radios - described as Samsung's **largest non-Korean RAN deal to date** and its **first extensive European footprint** with this group.
- An end-to-end Open RAN modernisation with **Liberty Puerto Rico**, Samsung's first full-scale Caribbean deployment, using 4G/5G Open RAN vRAN with AI-based automation and multi-band radios.
- Multi-country **Open RAN contracts with DISH in the US and Vodafone in Europe**, positioning Samsung as a lead commercial Open RAN supplier in greenfield and brownfield contexts.

Samsung also wins in **selected Indian and South-East Asian engagements** where it combines local manufacturing, PLI participation and custom radios, as in the 5G break-in at an Indian Tier-1 operator where it won about **10%** 5G RAN share.

Where Samsung **loses versus Ericsson**:

- In India, Samsung **failed to secure 5G RAN awards at a major operator where it was LTE incumbent**, with Ericsson winning by better aligning to the operator's requirements according to internal feedback.
- In the same market, Ericsson is recorded as winning roughly **50%** 5G RAN share at the operator where Samsung took 10%, highlighting our stronger position in high-scale deployments.
- Samsung's RAN revenue and shipment declines in 2024 are most pronounced at **KDDI, Verizon, Bharti and KT**, pointing to execution and competitiveness issues even in reference accounts.
- In the **VodafoneThree UK Open RAN replacement** project, Samsung is explicitly listed as having lost to competitors in an Open RAN context, despite its broader Open RAN credentials.

Ericsson CI attributes many of these losses to **TCO and power consumption** gaps, a **weaker LTE footprint**, and Samsung's heavier dependence on local service partners and custom hardware, all of which increase risk and lifecycle cost compared to Ericsson's SRAN and energy-optimised portfolio.

**Known tactics and plays**

Samsung's **recurring commercial and negotiation plays** observed in Ericsson CI include:

- **Low initial anchor price as a new entrant**: Samsung tends to enter bids with a comparatively low starting price and a flexible scope, then seeks to adjust HW/SW objects and commercial structure to reach desired margins.
- **Highly tailored, customer-led roadmaps**: it favours customer-driven feature development, funded by **annual SW development fees** per network, and positions customised SW as a differentiator that locks in the operator and justifies recurring fees.
- **Shifting value between HW and SW**: Samsung explicitly uses the ability to **move price objects between hardware and software** to fit customer perceptions, avoid direct feature price comparison and maximise monetisation.
- **ORAN and SA "future-proofing" narrative**: in markets where it lacks 4G footprint, Samsung pushes **SA architecture, open fronthaul and vRAN compliance** to reframe the decision around openness and future multi-vendor evolution rather than current coverage or TCO.
- **Custom radios and device ecosystem leverage**: it is willing to **build radios to operator-specific specifications** (for example in Korea, the US and India) and leverages its smartphone portfolio to support 5G adoption, especially via mass-market 5G devices in India and the Gulf.

In one high-profile Latin American tender, Samsung's bid is described as **"ruinous"** by a rival consortium due to a 5G core installation offer well below the operator's internal cost estimate and alleged gaps on interoperability requirements, triggering formal challenges and competitor attempts to have Samsung disqualified. This case reinforces the perception that Samsung is prepared to **use very aggressive pricing on strategic core elements** to disrupt incumbent vendors and redefine benchmarks, even at the cost of regulatory scrutiny.

**How Ericsson counters them**

Ericsson CI's **"How to Beat Samsung"** guidance clusters into **technical** and **commercial** counter-moves.

On **technical positioning**:

- Lead with **full multi-RAT SRAN support** (2G/3G/LTE/IoT/5G) and our significantly broader install base (around **100 live networks and 150+ commercial 5G contracts** cited) to de-risk migrations and feature evolution versus Samsung's more limited LTE and SRAN footprint.
- Quantify the **TCO advantage**: up to **20% lower TCO** for Ericsson driven by **up to 3x lower 5G power consumption** and requiring fewer sites or equipment units, especially in M-MIMO deployments.
- Emphasise **radio and M-MIMO strengths**: Ericsson is 25% ahead on remote radio footprint for similar output power and 10-15% better on radio power efficiency, with a broader range of high-performance M-MIMO products than Samsung's top-end portfolio.
- Highlight **roadmap robustness**: contrast Samsung's removal of the higher-capacity CDU60 and limited scale-up path with Ericsson's continued investment in high-capacity baseband, Massive MIMO and mmWave, including proven extended-range demos.

On **commercial and engagement tactics**:

- **Understand and expose Samsung's TCO structure**: use our knowledge that Samsung needs more equipment and has higher power consumption to translate its flexible price objects into tangible long-term OPEX and capex implications for the operator.
- **Influence RFx in our favour**: Ericsson CI stresses the need to **shape RFx requirements** early around multi-RAT, performance, energy efficiency, Open RAN maturity and lifecycle support, which amplify our strengths and make Samsung's gaps visible.
- **Anchor on commercial model clarity**: contrast our **transparent price objects** and well-defined SW models with Samsung's bundling and annual development fees, which can create uncertainty on future charges and roadmap commitments.
- **Position Open RAN pragmatically**: where Samsung pushes ORAN and vRAN, we counter by emphasising **performance, energy efficiency and integration** plus our own Open RAN compatibility, positioning Ericsson as the **primary value vendor** and Samsung as an optional challenger if the operator insists on a second source.

Ericsson CI explicitly advises that we should **not chase Samsung's most aggressive price plays at any cost**, especially where their TCO and operational risks remain high. The recommendation is to **compete on value structure and long-term ROI**, rather than headline price, and to walk away from engagements that cannot be shaped towards our strengths.

**Recent moves and watch-items**

Key **recent developments** and **watch-items** from the Ericsson CI corpus:

- **Open RAN and vRAN expansion**: Samsung has secured large Open RAN and vRAN deals with DISH in the US, Vodafone across multiple European markets, and KDDI in Japan, with deployments spanning tens of thousands of radios and multi-country footprints.
- **AI-driven network operations**: Samsung is actively rolling out AI-based Energy Saving Manager and Non-RT RIC, with field tests in Tier-1 US networks showing **15-35% energy savings** and reported throughput improvements, and a commercial RIC deployment in Canada.
- **6G and AI-RAN R&D**: it holds a chair role in 3GPP RAN and participates in the Verizon 6G Innovation Forum, while signing multiple MoUs with SK Telecom, KT and SoftBank on AI-RAN and 6G trials, including distributed MIMO and AI-based channel estimation.
- **India and manufacturing**: Samsung continues to invest in Indian manufacturing and PLI schemes, while expanding 5G deployments with Vodafone Idea using vRAN and AI-based SON, even as it faces a significant tax dispute related to historic 4G infrastructure.
- **Financial pressure**: networks revenue fell about **21-25%** in 2024, reaching its lowest level since 2011, and global RAN share dropped by about **1 percentage point** to **4.7%**, with particular weakness in North America and APAC. Samsung nonetheless guides for **RAN share gains in a flat market** driven by new Open RAN and vRAN wins, including Vodafone's "Spring" initiative in Europe.
- **Reputational and compliance risks**: a patent infringement case resulted in a **hundreds-of-millions USD** damages order on Samsung Electronics for 4G/5G/Wi-Fi patents, and emergency-call failures in older Samsung phones in Australia triggered regulatory scrutiny and operator blocking of affected devices. The Costa Rica tender challenge on pricing and interoperability adds another regulatory exposure point.
- **AI-ready COTS strategy**: Samsung is actively marketing an AI-ready network architecture that runs core, RAN and virtualised routers on COTS servers, aiming to cut CAPEX and energy while unifying data for AI orchestration. This is a key messaging pillar in 2026 marketing and influences how it will approach future RFx in our regions.

These moves indicate a **strategically ambitious but financially pressured challenger**: strong momentum in Open RAN, vRAN and AI-driven automation, but with structural TCO disadvantages versus Ericsson and increasing scrutiny around aggressive commercial tactics.
