**Competitive Intelligence - Huawei**

**Profile and position**

Huawei is a large-scale, end-to-end ICT vendor with a leading RAN and strong core position globally, anchored in China and expanded across emerging markets.  
In 2024 it generated roughly USD 120 billion in revenue, up about 22% year-on-year, with ICT Infrastructure as the largest segment and Carrier business a core contributor.  
Huawei invested around USD 25 billion in R&D in 2024, with more than half of employees in R&D, supporting sustained product and chipset development.

Huawei held about 35% of the worldwide RAN market in 2024, gaining a few points of share year-on-year, driven largely by APAC.  
Outside China it had roughly 25% RAN share, with a clear leadership position in Latin America at close to 50% RAN share and a robust RAN footprint in Europe.  
In mobile core it holds around 30% share worldwide ex-China and more than 50% in Latin America.

Huawei has no meaningful new activity in North America, where remaining installations are being swapped out, and faces ongoing replacement in EU markets subject to high-risk vendor policies.  
In Europe, Middle East, Africa, Latin America and parts of Asia it combines strong installed base, government relationships and aggressive commercial strategy to remain one of Ericsson's most competitive RAN and core rivals.

**Portfolio and technical position**

Huawei positions itself as an end-to-end solution provider across RAN, core, transport, antennas, energy and cloud, with a broad radio and Massive MIMO portfolio and high-capacity basebands.  
Ericsson internal assessment notes that Huawei differentiates via breadth of radio and mMIMO options and proprietary technologies, while Ericsson counters with multi-band radios, AI-driven basebands, better baseband power consumption and a more open Cloud RAN architecture.

Huawei is driving a 5.5G (5G-Advanced) narrative as an AI-centric evolution step towards 6G, combining software and hardware capabilities launched in 2024.  
It positions 5.5G as AI-driven cloud architecture with enhanced spectral efficiency, energy efficiency, sensing, positioning, and massive IoT connectivity, targeting industrial and advanced consumer use cases.

At the product level, Huawei has launched a full set of commercial 5.5G network equipment, including high-end MetaAAU and BladeAAU mMIMO radios, sub-1 GHz mMIMO, tri-band and ultra-light AAUs, 6 GHz AAUs and rural coverage solutions.  
The portfolio includes LampSite indoor solutions with passive IoT support and high uplink capacity for scenario-based deployments.

Huawei is investing heavily in RAN AI, adding Intelligent Fusion Boards and UISP compute boards into the BBU sub-rack to support service recognition, UX assurance, network optimisation and power-saving use cases, and to host local packet gateway functions.  
It also pushes Integrated Sensing and Communication (ISAC) with dedicated mMIMO AAUs, including new large-angle AAUs for low-altitude communication, priced at a premium to legacy 64T units.

The baseband architecture uses sub-racks with multiple board types and a fast hardware life cycle, requiring new boards every roughly 2-3 years and frequent board configuration changes aligned to software releases.  
Ericsson internal analysis highlights that Huawei needs multiple boards per site for configurations where Ericsson can use more flexible baseband units with DM/TM capability.

On Cloud RAN and openness, Ericsson assesses Huawei's AI/RAN architecture as conceptually similar two-layer OMC/node architecture but weaker on openness, while Ericsson emphasises its more open Cloud RAN positioning.  
On Open RAN, Huawei has lost at least one major opportunity where a European operator replaced several thousand Huawei 5G sites with a competitor in an Open RAN programme, and has faced regulatory pushback in tenders explicitly requiring Open RAN.

**Commercial and pricing behaviour**

Huawei prioritises customer-centricity and modularity in offers and is highly flexible in commercial structures, adapting to local market conditions and cost pressures.  
It plays a "volume game," guiding customer demand and relying on scale to sustain a flexible business model that requires sizeable market share.

Huawei shows high flexibility in cost representation: it reshapes Capex/Opex splits and TCO over term years to minimise perceived customer pain while achieving target margins.  
It uses granular capacity licensing across radio and baseband with small step sizes (bandwidth, MIMO layers, power) to enable linear pricing and avoid over-dimensioning.  
Licensing adds extra items for modes, bands and carriers, allowing upsell by unlocking latent hardware functionality over time.

Commercial tools include capacity-driven HW monetisation, basic vs optional software feature licensing, and staged monetisation that starts with stripped-down configurations and upsells capabilities later.  
Huawei typically sells current software versions and monetises future 5G-Advanced features, such as XR and RedCap, as incremental paid upgrades.

Huawei charges a premium for AI features and in some cases for AI outcomes, where operators pay based on measurable results delivered by AI.  
It also offers a Pay-As-You-Save green energy model where solar-powered RAN sites are financed from realised OPEX energy savings, enabling low-Capex decarbonisation.

The vendor is willing to engage in creative financial engineering, including deferred payments, vendor financing, and hybrid Capex/Opex models, and can adapt or co-create commercial models to match CSP budget constraints.  
Internal market input notes sole-supplier offers, vouchers on end-to-end scenarios, and a readiness to accept negative margins in initial contract terms in non-restricted markets to build long-term positions.

Recently Huawei has become less aggressive on vouchers and discounts for radio hardware, likely due to higher internal chipset costs, but remains aggressive where it pursues swaps and takes a harder line on discounts in pure expansion deals.

**Where they win and where they lose against Ericsson**

Huawei wins when it can combine broad portfolio, aggressive pricing, and flexible financing in cost-sensitive environments, especially where geopolitical restrictions are low and government relationships are strong.  
In Latin America and parts of Africa and Asia it has secured large 5G and modernisation contracts with major operators, often bundling Massive MIMO, multi-band radios, AI operations platforms and energy-efficiency narratives.

Huawei tends to outperform when it can guide demand early, leverage consultancy to shape RFPs, and present co-creation and profit-sharing models that position it as a business partner rather than a pure equipment supplier.  
It exploits pricing flexibility and rich licensing structures to align apparent TCO with customer budgets while keeping strong upsell paths.

Huawei loses where high-risk-vendor regulations apply, especially in North America and parts of Europe, where its equipment is being removed or swapped out.  
It has lost at least one major Open RAN-driven replacement in Europe where thousands of 5G sites were handed to another vendor, and faces further risk where open and multi-vendor architectures are mandated.

Ericsson analysis highlights technical and lifecycle weaknesses in Huawei's baseband architecture and board churn, as well as higher power consumption relative to Ericsson basebands, which can be exploited in TCO comparisons.  
In markets where "trusted vendor" positioning and security perceptions are decisive, Huawei is structurally disadvantaged and Ericsson can win on trust, compliance and transparency.

**Position at this customer and in region**

In the Middle East and Africa (including GCC), Huawei has a high network presence and leverages strong geopolitical positioning, deep customer relationships and influence.  
Market feedback in the region describes Huawei as very aggressive on TCO when attacking strategic markets or networks and already aggressive from the start of the sales cycle.

Huawei is perceived as more flexible than peers on T&Cs, deal currency, payment terms, warranties and service levels, and it responds quickly during tender phases with what appears to be a decentralised decision-making model.  
In strategic opportunities it can agree to non-standard requests such as free-of-charge modernisations to secure footprint.

Huawei maintains supply hubs close to selected African customers to shorten time-to-market, and uses strong services and managed operations presence to gain operational insight, which it then exploits to promote its own portfolio and highlight incumbent weaknesses.  
In some markets in North East Asia Ericsson is actively targeting replacement of parts of Huawei's installed base, showing that displacement is possible where policy and perception allow it.

No specific internal view is stated in these sources on Huawei's exact footprint at a given leading GCC operator, but the general MEA patterns above apply to similar customers in the region.

**Known tactics and plays**

Huawei runs continuous business and network consultancy to map technical needs and commercial capacity, then designs financially viable solutions that position its portfolio and shape RFPs before they are issued.  
It keeps detailed internal competitive intelligence, enabling local offices to tailor offers to directly address competitor weaknesses and urgent customer needs.

The vendor applies a holistic approach to every opportunity, routinely bundling RAN (HW, SW and management), antennas, transport, core, energy, AI processing and ISAC into integrated proposals, often incentivised with vouchers that scale with bundle size.  
It uses sole-supplier offers, end-to-end vouchers, and long-term views on sales, including accepting negative margins early, particularly in markets without HRV restrictions.

Huawei's licensing design allows multiple monetisation levers: activating additional modes, bands or carriers; scaling Massive MIMO layers; selling optional high-value features; and charging for AI features or results.  
It protects revenue by having fallback upsell strategies if the full planned solution cannot be sold, for example monetising smaller features or AI operations when additional spectrum or carriers do not materialise.

Huawei leverages strong CPQ tooling (I-sense) with cloudified configuration-quotation and integrated approval workflows, enabling full offer configuration and end-to-end offer submission with bid response times around four to five days.  
Market input characterises some tactics as aggressive and at times unscrupulous, requiring Ericsson to tighten monitoring and protection against information leaks.

It uses managed services and network design and optimisation roles to obtain competitive intelligence, amplifies incumbent performance weaknesses, and positions its niche portfolio as tailored responses to those gaps.  
Once it identifies a competitive edge, Huawei can become firm on terms and TCO, even after initially offering generous incentives.

**How Ericsson counters them**

Ericsson internal guidance stresses building strong, trust-based relationships and using "trusted vendor" positioning, particularly as this concept moves from Europe into other regions such as Middle East and Africa.  
It advises incumbents to proactively drive modernisation while still holding footprint, anticipating customer needs to reduce the likelihood of fully open tenders.

Commercially, Ericsson is advised to avoid box-to-box comparisons and instead drive solution- and consultancy-level value discussions focused on tangible business outcomes, revenue opportunities, OPEX and energy savings.  
TCO comparisons at solution level should highlight Ericsson advantages in baseband power consumption and lifecycle, and expose "hidden costs and risks" in Huawei's bundling and licensing strategies.

Ericsson should counter voucher-driven bundling with transparent, TCO-based pricing and clear value-based or capacity-based licensing, and push practical 5G and 5G-Advanced use cases supported by demos.  
Guidance calls for holistic offers that cover both main scope and uncovered gaps in the same proposal, while extending scope beyond RFx minimums where that strengthens Ericsson's position.

To face Huawei's offer flexibility, Ericsson is advised to work more closely with Market Areas, with enhanced authority to adjust offer components, improve internal alignment across product divisions, and allow external price flexibility without undermining long-term margin integrity.  
It should also factor Huawei's potential financial support (bank incentives, long-term loans, instalments, deferred and ROI-linked payments) into deal strategy.

Operationally, Ericsson should include monitoring of access to its equipment and technology in deals to protect against competitive intelligence breaches, and raise awareness and training for local engagement and services teams.

**Recent moves and watch-items**

Recent internal CI highlights Huawei's intensified focus on AI-enhanced 5.5G, AI-native network architectures and aggressive global market expansion, particularly in Latin America, Africa and selected Asian markets.  
Notable recent wins include large 5G and modernisation contracts with major operators in Indonesia, Latin America and Brazil, combining MetaAAU and BladeAAU Massive MIMO, multi-band radios and AI-based automation platforms.

Financially, group revenue grew strongly in 2024 with ICT Infrastructure and Consumer devices both expanding, supported by very high R&D spend and a revenue mix dominated by China.  
RAN share gains globally and ex-China, plus core leadership in CALA, signal that Huawei remains a primary competitive threat wherever geopolitics does not constrain it.

At the same time, regulatory and policy actions in Europe and North America continue to tighten, with bans, phase-outs and funding restrictions for Huawei equipment, and expanded US sanctions on advanced chips and export controls.  
Future watch-items include progression of 5.5G and AI-RAN deployments, further large-scale swaps or removals driven by HRV policy, Huawei's ability to sustain R&D under sanctions, and continued use of sophisticated licensing, bundling and financing plays in MEA and Latin America.
